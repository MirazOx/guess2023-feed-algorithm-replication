Methodological replication · computational social science

Does a chronological feed change how people think?

A synthetic-data reconstruction of Guess, A. M., et al. (2023), How do social media feed algorithms affect attitudes and behavior in an election campaign? Science 381, 398. 10.1126/science.abp9364

Read this first: these numbers are from synthetic data

The original study's data is restricted-access and cannot be obtained. This report reconstructs the study's analysis pipeline and runs it on a simulated dataset in which the paper's reported effect sizes were deliberately planted as "ground truth." The goal is to show that the method recovers those effects and reproduces the study's pattern — not to estimate Facebook's real effects. Treat every number here as a demonstration of method.

The question, and the surprising answer

In autumn 2020, around 23,000 consenting Facebook users were randomly assigned to keep their normal algorithmic feed or to switch to a simple reverse-chronological feed (newest posts first) for three months, spanning the US presidential election. The researchers then asked: did changing the feed change people's politics?

The headline answer surprised many observers. Switching to the chronological feed dramatically changed what people saw and reduced what they did on the platform — but left their political attitudes essentially unchanged.

What changed

The feed showed more political content, political news, content from untrustworthy sources, and content from across the aisle. And on-platform political engagement fell.

What didn't change

Affective polarization, issue polarization, political knowledge, news knowledge, self-reported participation, and turnout intentions — all unmoved over the study window.

An important caveat the authors themselves flag

Facebook's ranking algorithm was in flux during the study because of 2020 election "break-glass" emergency measures, so the control group's "algorithmic feed" was not a stable baseline (noted in a 2024 Science editorial). Any replication inherits this limitation.

How the study worked (in plain language)

Design A randomised experiment. Because people were assigned to feeds at random, any later difference between the groups can be attributed to the feed rather than to who they already were.

Sample ~23,000 Facebook "study-completes" who finished the surveys (plus a parallel Instagram sample). Treatment reverse-chronological feed for ~3 months. Measurement what their feed contained, what they did on-platform, and survey scales for attitudes — measured before and after.

How this replication works

Three steps, all documented in the project's notebook and code:

1. Simulate. Build a synthetic dataset with the same structure — treatment arms, randomisation within strata, an ~12% compliance glitch (a real web bug in the original), and survey weights — and plant the paper's reported effect sizes.

2. Estimate. Rebuild the paper's own statistical recipe: a regression-adjusted average-treatment-effect estimator (Lin, 2013), robust standard errors, data-driven control selection (lasso), a weighted population estimate (PATE) versus an unweighted sample estimate (SATE), and a multiple-comparison correction (sharpened false-discovery-rate) so that testing many outcomes doesn't manufacture false positives.

3. Check. Confirm the estimator returns the planted effects and the study's qualitative pattern — and that this holds across several random seeds, so it isn't a fluke.

What this does and doesn't show

It does show that the paper's analysis pipeline is sound and reproducible, and that its qualitative story — strong first stage, null attitudes, real behavioural effect — falls naturally out of that pipeline.

It does not show anything about Facebook's real effects: the data is synthetic, the magnitudes are planted, and the simplifications (modelled weights, coarser strata, skipped scale-construction, the algorithm-in-flux caveat) are all listed honestly in the project's replication_log.md.
